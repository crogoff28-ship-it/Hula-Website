About Us

Our Mission

Our purpose is to spread the values of hula around the islands and to preserve Hawaiian culture and history through storytelling. We also want to help foster a deeply connected relationship with the environment and grow their ancestral knowledge. Additionally, we believe hula helps promote both physical and mental well-being as it helps us calm ourselves through dance. In other words, hula is an expression without words that comes from the heart. Rather than a traditional dance class, hula is the transfer of wisdom from kumu hula (master teacher) to their haumana (students) with an equal form of respect and interconnectedness with the ‘āina.

Origin & Background

Hula is as ancient as the Hawaiian people themselves, and many legends explain its origin. Some traditions believe Laka, the navigator goddess, invented hula; others state Hi’iaka created the dance for her sister Pele. Some say Pele herself, the volcano and fire goddess, created hula. Although many more theories and myths exist, they all reflect the same idea inherent to Hawaiian culture: the art of hula holds power. From storytelling, spiritual beliefs, connectedness, and knowledge, hula is deeply rooted in the people of Hawaii.

Banning hula in 1830 by the Westerners didn't stop the Hawaiians from practicing their hula in secret, but still left the Hawaiians silenced and feeling powerless. King David Kalākaua restored public hula performances in the late 1800s through the Merrie Monarch. For better or for worse, the Hawaiian culture was changing, and therefore, new hybrid styles of hula emerged. Contact with the Westerners, cultural exchange, and changing Hawaiian society all play a role in the evolution of Hula.

Over time, hula spread globally, reaching communities worldwide, sharing Hawaiian traditions. It is crucial that hula remains authentic to its cultural values and embodies truth, wisdom, and celebration. Hula continues to evolve, carrying the stories of past generations as it honors where it began.

Kumu

Kumu Naomi, a well-respected kumu hula at Hanalei School, spoke to me about the humility, grace, and honor required to teach the next generation of hula. To be a kumu means remaining open to learning and understanding the culture and gravity of what it means to practice hula. Hula itself helps foster a deeply interconnected relationship with the land and culture. Hula, the Hawaiians' storytelling through motion, has trudged onward due to the great kumus before us that have helped the poeple everywhere connect with their culture, find a sense of identity, and create a relationship with the land and nature.

Being a kumu requires more than a mastery of a skill, but also an artistry of leadership and an ablility to guide others toward the right paths. Earning the title of kumu is a noble accomplishment. Kumus hold the responsibility of passing on and sharing wisdom and knowledge of their own teachers while still embodying their own presence.
